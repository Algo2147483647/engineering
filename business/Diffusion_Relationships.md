# Diffusion Relationships

Referral-based diffusion relationships refer to a relationship attribution and progress computation mechanism designed for growth-oriented scenarios. In such a mechanism, a user acts as a `leader` and initiates a `diffusion instance`, within a specific business context (`scene`). The system then generates a shareable access point (`share code`), such as a link, invitation code, or referral code. When other users, referred to as `followers`, enter through these access points, the system attributes their participation to the corresponding leader and diffusion instance, and persists this participation as a relationship record (`relation`). After validity checks and contribution measurement, these relationship records are further used to update instance-level progress and support downstream business policy decisions.

The mechanism serves three core purposes:

1. recording a diffusion activity;
2. recording relationships between users; and
3. making these relationships available to business applications.

> **Initiation of diffusion → Instance creation → Access point generation → User participation → Attribution → Relationship persistence → Validity and contribution assessment → Progress update → Policy output**

In growth strategies such as new-user invitation, friend-assisted campaigns, group buying, referral codes, sharing-based rebates, and member-get-member programs, businesses need to record and manage relationship data generated through user-driven diffusion. They also need to use these relationships to support business policy decisions.

This mechanism abstracts fragmented and often inconsistent diffusion paths into a stable relationship model and progress computation framework. From the user perspective, it makes the participation process transparent: who initiated the activity, through which access point a user joined, whether the participation is valid, what the current progress is, and whether the conditions for rewards or group formation have been satisfied. From the business perspective, it provides a unified foundation for relationship records and progress computation, enabling different growth mechanisms to reuse the same underlying capabilities.

## Contribution Recognition and Settlement

In referral-driven growth systems, a referral record should not be interpreted simply as "*A invited B*". Rather, it indicates that A generated propagation evidence that reached B, thereby establishing only a potential attribution claim. The substantive question is "*whether the business should recognize the underlying propagation action as a growth contribution and, if so, how that contribution should be valued, settled, and credited*". Thus, after the initial evidence is captured, the record must pass through a sequence of rule-based attribution and eligibility decisions. A more precise formulation is:

> *A provisional contribution claim is created for A with respect to B under scenario X and rule Y.*

Referral-driven growth flows are inherently ambiguous. A participant may enter through multiple channels, and a single participation event may match multiple campaign instances. For this reason, a contribution should not be recognized as valid at the moment propagation evidence is captured. A cleaner architecture separates the process into a factual layer and a decision layer. The factual layer records observable events, whereas the decision layer determines whether those events are recognized under business rules and what growth value, if any, they generate.

A typical contribution lifecycle consists of the following stages:

> **propagation evidence collection → candidate relationship generation → relational attribution → eligibility validation → contribution valuation → benefit settlement**

1. **Propagation evidence collection**: During propagation evidence collection, the system records evidence of both propagation and participation, preserving the factual basis required for subsequent decisions.
2. **Candidate Relationship Generation**: When propagation evidence is linked to a participant’s action, the system creates a candidate relationship record. This record serves as the input to the decision process and should preserve the original signals.
3. **Relational Attribution**. Relational attribution determines which referrer, campaign instance, and rule set should be associated with the participation event; in effect, it answers the question of who should receive credit.
4. **Eligibility Validation**. Once attribution has been resolved, eligibility validation determines whether the attributed relationship satisfies the business rules required for recognition. Only after passing this stage can a candidate relationship be promoted to a recognized contribution.
5. **Contribution Valuation**. Contribution valuation then computes the value generated by the recognized contribution.
6. **Benefit Settlement**. Benefit settlement applies the computed result to the relevant business object, such as the progress state of a campaign instance. Once settlement is complete, the relationship state should be finalized and remain fully auditable.

### Relational Attribution

**Relation Uniqueness.** Relation uniqueness defines the business scope within which a relation is considered unique: within a given scope, the same follower may establish only one active relation. The system should allowing different business rules to determine the uniqueness boundary. Such constraints may be defined along one or more of the following dimensions:

- **Scenario- or campaign-level uniqueness:** constrains participation by the same subject within a specific business scenario or marketing campaign, such as allowing a user to participate in a campaign only once.
- **Propagation-instance-level uniqueness:** constrains participation by the same subject within a single propagation instance, such as a group-buying session, assistance task, sharing instance, or propagation chain, where participation is allowed only once.
- **Time-window-based frequency control:** constrains the participation frequency of the same subject within a specified time window, such as allowing participation only once per cycle.
- **Subject-based uniqueness scope:** specifies the entity around which the uniqueness constraint is enforced. The subject may be a follower, leader, account, device, phone number, membership identity, merchant, team, or another business entity.

**User Identity Resolution.** In referral-based diffusion scenarios, the establishment of diffusion relationships may precede the formation of a stable user identity. For example, in referral acquisition or new-user invitation scenarios, a Follower may enter the diffusion chain through an invitation code before completing platform registration. In practice, a user’s identity may also evolve through multiple stages, such as a temporary visitor identity, a logged-in identity, a registered-user identity, and an order-related identity. The system therefore needs to maintain continuity across different identity states while the user’s identity is still unstable or undergoing change. It must continuously recognize the same real-world participant and accurately attribute that participant’s subsequent business activities to the original diffusion entry point, Leader, and Diffusion Instance. This capability can be achieved by combining deterministic and semi-deterministic identity signals, including device identifiers, application-instance identifiers, session identifiers, login accounts, registered accounts, and order accounts, together with feature-association techniques.
